Artistic Currency in West Africa

Bronze and gold were deeply intertwined in West African coastal and inland economies. Along the Gulf of Guinea, bronze was closely linked to gold, especially among peoples like the Atti√©, Baoul√©, Agni (C√¥te d‚ÄôIvoire) and the Ashanti (Ghana). Gold dust was used as currency, and bronze was the material used to cast the famed goldweights, jewelry and other artistic and social expressions.

Though typically associated with gold measurement, Akan goldweights (abrammuo) hold a crucial place in the larger ecosystem of West African bronze artistry. Their aesthetic complexity and symbolic density connect them to broader West African bronze traditions. Like the serpentine motifs of the Gan or the animal amulets of the Lobi, Akan weights reflect a culture where metalwork was visual language. In this broader context, goldweights represent a refined microcosm of West African bronze culture, simultaneously utilitarian and deeply expressive.

These miniature sculptures are visual narratives, embedding proverbs, moral lessons, and cultural values into metal. Their small size contrasts with their artistic sophistication; a single goldweight can embody an entire worldview, whether through the stylized spiral of a geometric form or the carefully modeled body of an animal or ancestor figure. Today, they are preserved and displayed in major museums worldwide as both historical tools and works of art, appreciated for their technical mastery and rich symbolism.
